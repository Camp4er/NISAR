🛰️ NISAR Mission
What is NISAR?
NISAR (NASA-ISRO Synthetic Aperture Radar) is a joint Earth observation satellite. It uses L-band and S-band SAR to measure surface deformation with millimeter precision globally.
Why L-band?
L-band radar (23.8 cm wavelength) penetrates cloud cover, smoke, and dense vegetation — critical for Northeast India where optical satellites are blocked by cloud and forest canopy.
NISAR vs Sentinel-1
Sentinel-1 uses C-band (5.5 cm) with limited vegetation penetration. NISAR's L-band is far superior for forested remote regions like the Shillong Plateau.
🌏 The Oldham Fault
What is it?
A major reverse fault along the southern edge of the Shillong Plateau. It caused the 1897 Great Assam Earthquake (M8.1) — one of the largest ever recorded.
Current Status
The fault remains locked, continuously accumulating stress from the India-Eurasia tectonic collision. One of India's highest seismic risk zones.
Why Monitor?
Over 10 million people live within the potential impact zone. Continuous InSAR monitoring is the only reliable way to track strain accumulation in this remote region.
🔬 Methodology
Data Pipeline
ARIA S1 GUNW → h5py extraction → coherence masking (≥0.5) → phase-to-displacement conversion → time series analysis → risk assessment
Displacement Formula
LOS Displacement (cm) = (Unwrapped Phase ÷ 4π) × Wavelength × 100
Magnitude Estimation
Wells & Coppersmith (1994): M = 4.07 + 0.98 × log(Rupture Area km²)
⚠️ Limitations
Cannot Predict Earthquakes
No system can reliably predict exact earthquake timing. This monitors risk indicators and provides hazard assessment — not prediction.
Atmospheric Noise
Water vapor can mimic deformation signals. Operational systems apply atmospheric correction (ERA5/GACOS) to separate real deformation from noise.
12-Day Revisit
Current data has 12-day gaps. NISAR's eventual 6-day revisit will significantly improve continuous monitoring capability.
